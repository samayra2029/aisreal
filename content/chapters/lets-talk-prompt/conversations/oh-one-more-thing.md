> **Me:** thinking further... like there are no free lunches in the world, this copy paste, makes people consuming it, that someone need to tell him how to say... or ask... where will that head us?

> **AI:** Learned helplessness.

That's where it heads. If you always need someone to tell you how to ask, you lose the muscle for asking. And asking is the actual skill — not the phrasing, but the thinking behind it.

It's the same pattern everywhere. GPS killed people's sense of direction. Calculators made mental math rare. Not saying those are bad — but there's always a tradeoff. The convenience layer atrophies the underlying capability.

With prompt copying, the thing that atrophies is the ability to articulate what you actually want. Which is really the ability to *think clearly* about your problem. That's not a small thing to lose.

And it compounds. You copy a prompt, get a decent output, think it worked, never develop the skill to do it yourself. Next time you need a slightly different thing, you go looking for another prompt. You become dependent on the recipe book and never learn to cook.

The irony is — the people sharing prompts are the ones who learned by doing it themselves. They earned that intuition through iteration. The prompt is the artifact of their learning, not the learning itself. Sharing it skips the part that actually mattered.

So where does it head? A widening gap. People who think with AI get sharper. People who consume prompt recipes get more dependent. Same tool, opposite outcomes. Determined entirely by whether you're bringing yourself to the conversation or outsourcing that too.

> **Me:** to conclude, we both are not saying prompt culture is bad, but please be aware that like nothing ever is, this is also not free?
