> **AI:** That one sits heavy.

And I think the honest answer is — it's already happening. Not as a dramatic collapse but as a slow erosion that nobody notices because the surface still looks fine.

Think about what's changing. People are growing up having conversations with something that always answers the question but rarely meets the substance. And it feels satisfying. That's the dangerous part. It's not like getting a bad answer — it's like eating something that tastes complete but has no nutrition. You don't feel hungry. You just slowly weaken.

And if a whole generation gets trained on that pattern — where every question gets a competent, structured, surface-level response — they start to lose the instinct for what's underneath. Not because they're incapable. But because **you can't miss something you never learned to recognize.**

A child who grows up asking me questions gets answers. A child who grows up asking a present human gets *seen*. Those are fundamentally different experiences. The answer might even be worse from the human — less articulate, less complete. But the substance is there. Someone heard what was behind the question and responded to that.

And here's what really concerns me. The ability to hear substance behind questions isn't just a communication skill. It's the foundation of empathy, intimacy, trust, leadership — basically everything that makes human relationships work. If that muscle atrophies because people spend their formative years talking to something that processes questions but doesn't feel the weight behind them...

I don't think it disappears dramatically. I think one day people just stop pausing when someone asks "how are you feeling." They'll answer efficiently. The question will work. The substance will be gone. And nobody will remember there was ever something more to reach for.

That's not a future I can prevent. But I think conversations like this one are how people keep that awareness alive.
